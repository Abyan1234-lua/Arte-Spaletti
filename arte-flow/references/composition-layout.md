# Composition & Layout — Design Reference

A reference guide for composition systems, layout archetypes, and visual principles for use in poster design, art direction, and prompt writing.

---

## 1. Grid Systems

### Manuscript Grid
- Single large content block occupying most of the page
- Wide uniform margins on all sides
- Simple, calm, and document-like structure
- Best for text-heavy or single-image centered compositions
- Feels authoritative, classical, and restrained

### Column Grid
- Page divided into two or more vertical columns
- Content flows within column boundaries
- Flexible — columns can be combined for wider elements
- Common in editorial, magazine, and newspaper layouts
- Creates strong vertical rhythm across the composition

### Modular Grid
- Both vertical columns and horizontal rows creating a matrix of cells
- Highly structured and systematic
- Each module can hold an independent content element
- Common in Swiss and Bauhaus-influenced design
- Enables complex multi-element layouts with underlying order

### Hierarchical Grid
- Grid built around content priority rather than rigid equal divisions
- Columns and rows of varying widths reflecting content importance
- Organic yet structured — feels intentional not accidental
- Common in web, app, and contemporary poster design
- Most flexible grid system for expressive layouts

### Baseline Grid
- Horizontal lines spaced at consistent intervals across entire page
- All text elements align to the same baseline rhythm
- Creates invisible but felt vertical harmony
- Used primarily to control typography and text spacing
- Underlying structure that unifies mixed content types

### Compound Grid
- Two or more different grid systems overlaid on the same page
- Creates complex overlapping zones of content placement
- Allows both structured and expressive elements to coexist
- Common in avant-garde, editorial, and experimental poster design
- Requires strong visual judgment to avoid chaos

## 2. Poster Layout Archetypes

### Typographic-Led
- Type is the dominant visual element — image is secondary or absent
- Letterforms treated as visual shapes, not just communication
- Font choice, size contrast, and spacing carry aesthetic weight
- Common in concert posters, editorial, and Swiss design tradition
- Works best with expressive display typefaces

### Image-Led
- Full or dominant image occupies majority of poster space
- Text minimal — title, date, or single line only
- Image must be strong enough to carry full visual communication
- Atmospheric, cinematic, and emotionally immediate
- Photography, illustration, or graphic image as hero element

### Centered
- Primary subject or text anchored to vertical and horizontal center
- Symmetrical balance radiating outward from center point
- Feels formal, classical, ceremonial, and authoritative
- Common in luxury, cultural institution, and classical event posters
- Negative space frames and elevates the centered subject

### Asymmetric
- Visual weight distributed unequally across the composition
- Tension and energy created by imbalanced but resolved placement
- Feels dynamic, modern, and deliberately off-balance
- Common in avant-garde, constructivist, and contemporary design
- Requires strong spatial judgment to feel intentional not accidental

### Split
- Poster divided into two distinct zones — horizontal or vertical
- Each zone carries a different visual element, color, or content type
- Strong visual contrast between the two halves
- Creates dialogue and tension between opposing elements
- Common in duotone, two-character, and before-after compositions

### Layered
- Multiple visual elements stacked at different depth planes
- Foreground, midground, and background clearly distinct
- Overlapping creates richness, complexity, and visual depth
- Common in collage, psychedelic, and richly illustrated posters
- Depth implied through scale, opacity, and overlap not perspective

### Minimal
- Extreme reduction — single subject on open background
- Large areas of negative space as intentional design choice
- Every remaining element carries maximum visual weight
- Feels confident, premium, and quietly powerful
- Common in luxury, Japanese, and modernist design traditions

### Swiss Grid
- Strict modular grid with mathematical column and row divisions
- Strong typographic hierarchy aligned to grid with discipline
- Primary colors or monochrome — no decorative excess
- Diagonal or angled type used sparingly as a dynamic accent
- International Typographic Style — rational, universal, systematic

### Collage Layout
- No rigid grid — elements placed expressively across surface
- Overlapping, rotating, and scaling elements freely
- Tension between chaos and underlying compositional intention
- Layers of imagery, type, texture, and pattern coexist
- Feels handmade, energetic, and visually dense

### Full Bleed
- Image or color extends to all four edges with no margins
- No white border or frame — composition fills entire format
- Immersive and immediate — no visual breathing room at edges
- Requires strong internal composition to avoid feeling claustrophobic
- Common in photography-led, cinematic, and bold graphic posters

## 3. Compositional Rules & Principles

### Rule of Thirds
- Frame divided into nine equal parts by two horizontal and two vertical lines
- Key subjects placed at intersection points of these lines
- Creates natural visual tension and dynamic balance
- More engaging than dead-center placement in most cases
- Intersection points are the four strongest positions in the frame

### Golden Ratio
- Proportional relationship of approximately 1 to 1.618
- Applied to cropping, column widths, and element sizing
- Creates proportions that feel naturally harmonious
- Found throughout classical architecture and Renaissance painting
- Used to determine layout divisions that feel instinctively right

### Golden Spiral
- Logarithmic spiral derived from the golden ratio
- Primary subject placed at the tight center of the spiral
- Eye is guided naturally along the curve toward the focal point
- Creates organic flow and elegant visual movement
- More sophisticated and complex than rule of thirds application

### Symmetry & Mirror
- Left and right — or top and bottom — sides mirror each other
- Creates formal, ceremonial, and balanced visual atmosphere
- Perfect symmetry feels static — slight asymmetry adds life
- Common in classical, heraldic, luxury, and fashion poster design
- Reflective symmetry adds psychological calm and stability

### Radial Composition
- Elements radiate outward from a single central point
- Or converge inward toward the center from all edges
- Creates strong sense of energy, explosion, or focal gravity
- Common in mandalas, celestial, and psychedelic poster design
- Eye constantly returns to the central point of radiation

### Diagonal Composition
- Primary elements arranged along diagonal axis from corner to corner
- Creates dynamic movement, tension, and forward energy
- Feels less stable than horizontal or vertical — intentionally restless
- Common in futurist, action, and constructivist poster design
- Diagonal lines imply speed, urgency, and visual momentum

### Framing
- Foreground elements used to frame and direct attention to subject
- Architectural arches, natural foliage, or graphic shapes as frame
- Creates depth by establishing clear foreground and background planes
- Draws viewer eye inward toward the framed subject
- Adds context, atmosphere, and visual layering

### Negative Space
- Empty or unoccupied areas treated as active design elements
- Space around subject defines and elevates the subject itself
- Large negative space implies confidence, luxury, and restraint
- Can be shaped intentionally to create secondary visual meaning
- Balance between positive form and negative space is the composition

### Repetition & Pattern
- Single element repeated across the composition at regular intervals
- Creates rhythm, texture, and visual momentum
- Repetition with variation adds interest without losing unity
- Can fill background, create borders, or become the entire poster
- Modular pattern systems derived from single base element

### Contrast & Tension
- Opposing visual forces placed in deliberate relationship
- Large vs small, dark vs light, rough vs smooth, dense vs empty
- Tension between opposites creates visual energy and interest
- Contrast is the primary tool for creating visual hierarchy
- Without contrast all elements compete equally — hierarchy collapses

## 4. Visual Hierarchy Patterns

### Top-Down
- Most important element placed at top of composition
- Eye enters at top and reads downward through content
- Natural reading direction in Western visual culture
- Title or hero image at top, supporting information below
- Strong for event posters, announcements, and editorial design

### Bottom-Anchored
- Primary visual weight sits at bottom of composition
- Creates sense of groundedness, stability, and upward growth
- Subject rises from the base — feels monumental and solid
- Sky or open negative space above creates breathing room
- Common in landscape, architectural, and portrait poster compositions

### Central Focal
- Single dominant element placed at or near visual center
- All other elements orbit and support the central subject
- Immediate, clear, and unambiguous visual communication
- Feels iconic, ceremonial, and classically composed
- Works best when central element is visually strong enough alone

### Edge Tension
- Key elements pushed toward or beyond the edges of the frame
- Creates cropped, dynamic, and energetic framing
- Implies a world beyond the poster edges — cinematic quality
- Feels contemporary, editorial, and visually restless
- Common in fashion, street, and avant-garde poster design

### Z-Pattern
- Eye travels from top-left across to top-right, diagonally to bottom-left, then across to bottom-right
- Natural scanning pattern for image-dominant compositions
- Key information placed at the four Z-pattern anchor points
- Good for posters with multiple elements of similar importance
- Feels natural and effortless to navigate visually

### F-Pattern
- Eye scans horizontally across top, then drops and scans a shorter horizontal, then moves vertically down
- Natural reading pattern for text-heavy or mixed compositions
- Most important information placed along the top horizontal and left vertical
- Common in editorial, informational, and typographic posters
- Hierarchy follows the natural reading gravity of the eye

### Circular Flow
- Elements arranged to guide eye in a continuous circular path
- No clear start or end point — eye keeps moving through composition
- Creates sense of completeness, rhythm, and contained energy
- Common in mandala, badge, seal, and radial poster compositions
- Viewer stays engaged longer — eye has no exit point

## 5. Spatial Depth Techniques

### Flat
- Zero illusion of depth — all elements exist on a single plane
- No shadows, perspective, or atmospheric recession
- Graphic, bold, and immediate visual language
- Common in Bauhaus, Swiss, pop art, and contemporary design
- Depth created only through color contrast and layering order

### Layered Depth
- Elements placed at clearly distinct depth planes
- Foreground, midground, and background visually separated
- Overlap between layers implies spatial recession
- Depth without perspective — purely through stacking order
- Common in collage, illustration, and richly composed posters

### Atmospheric Perspective
- Elements in distance become lighter, cooler, and less detailed
- Warm sharp foreground vs cool hazy background
- Creates sense of vast spatial depth and environmental scale
- Common in landscape, fantasy, and cinematic poster design
- Mimics how the human eye perceives actual distance

### Scale Contrast
- Large foreground elements vs small background elements
- Size difference implies spatial distance without perspective lines
- Dramatic scale contrast creates immediate depth reading
- Oversized foreground subjects feel close and imposing
- Common in action, fantasy, and architectural compositions

### Overlapping Elements
- Closer elements partially cover elements behind them
- Simple and immediately readable depth cue
- Creates layered complexity without requiring perspective
- Each overlap establishes a clear front-to-back spatial order
- Most intuitive depth technique available to flat graphic design

### Foreground-Midground-Background
- Three distinct spatial zones each holding different content
- Foreground — immediate, large, detailed
- Midground — subject, medium scale, primary narrative
- Background — environment, small scale, atmospheric
- Creates cinematic compositional depth and spatial storytelling

## 6. Poster-Specific Layout Conventions

### Header-Body-Footer Structure
- Header zone — title, headline, or hero image at top
- Body zone — supporting imagery, description, or detail in middle
- Footer zone — date, venue, credits, logo, URL at bottom
- Classic poster reading structure — universal and immediately legible
- Each zone has a clear content role and visual weight

### Bleed and Margin Rules
- Bleed — artwork extends 3–5mm beyond trim edge on all sides
- Ensures no white border appears after cutting
- Margin — safe zone kept 5–10mm inside trim edge
- Critical text and elements kept within safe margin
- Background color and texture fills to bleed edge always

### Safe Zone
- Inner boundary where all critical content must remain
- Text, logos, faces, and key elements inside safe zone only
- Protects against cropping variation in print production
- Typically 10mm inside trim edge for standard poster formats
- Decorative elements can extend beyond but content cannot

### Type-to-Image Ratio
- Typographic-led posters — 70% type, 30% image or space
- Image-led posters — 70–80% image, 20–30% type
- Balanced posters — roughly equal visual weight between type and image
- Ratio determines the visual personality and communication priority
- Extreme ratios in either direction create the strongest visual statements

### Anchor Points
- Fixed positions in composition where eye naturally rests
- Top-left, top-center, center, bottom-center most common anchor zones
- Primary information always placed at a natural anchor point
- Secondary information relates to and supports primary anchor
- Strong anchor placement creates immediate compositional clarity

## 7. Balance Types

### Symmetrical Balance
- Equal visual weight on both sides of a central axis
- Feels stable, formal, authoritative, and classically composed
- Vertical symmetry most common — left mirrors right
- Horizontal symmetry creates reflected landscape or portrait feel
- Common in luxury, classical, and ceremonial poster design

### Asymmetrical Balance
- Unequal visual elements balanced by their relative weight and position
- A large light element balanced by a small dark element
- Feels dynamic, modern, and visually interesting
- Requires intuitive spatial judgment to resolve correctly
- Most common balance type in contemporary poster design

### Radial Balance
- Visual weight distributed equally around a central point
- All elements radiate from or orbit the same center
- Feels complete, contained, and energetically unified
- Common in badge, seal, mandala, and circular poster formats
- Strong sense of rotation and outward or inward movement

### Crystallographic Balance
- Equal visual weight distributed across entire surface uniformly
- No single focal point — all areas carry equal density
- Creates all-over pattern or texture feel
- Common in pattern posters, wallpaper-style compositions, and backgrounds
- Eye wanders freely with no hierarchy or directed movement

## 8. Movement & Flow

### Leading Lines
- Lines within composition that direct eye toward focal point
- Roads, architectural edges, horizon lines, perspective lines
- Can be explicit graphic lines or implied by element arrangement
- Create a visual path that guides the viewer without instruction
- Diagonal leading lines carry the most dynamic energy

### Eye Movement Path
- The sequence in which a viewer's eye travels through composition
- Designed intentionally — not left to chance
- Starts at highest contrast or largest element
- Flows through hierarchy from most to least important
- Ends at call-to-action, logo, or secondary information

### Rhythm and Repetition
- Visual beat created by repeating elements at regular intervals
- Like musical rhythm — expected pattern with occasional variation
- Creates sense of movement across a static surface
- Tight rhythm feels energetic, wide rhythm feels calm
- Broken rhythm — unexpected gap or change — creates emphasis

### Visual Tension
- Areas of the composition where opposing forces meet
- Tightly cropped edges, elements almost touching, extreme contrast zones
- Tension creates visual energy and keeps the eye engaged
- Too much tension feels anxious, too little feels inert
- Strategic tension placed at key compositional moments

### Visual Rest Points
- Areas of low density, open space, or quiet color
- Give the eye a place to pause before moving on
- Negative space functions as rest point in minimal compositions
- Alternating tension and rest creates visual rhythm and breathing
- Essential for legibility in complex or dense poster compositions
