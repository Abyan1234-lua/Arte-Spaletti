# Art Styles — Design Characteristics Reference

A reference guide for 15 classic art styles, covering core visual traits for use in art direction, prompt writing, and graphic design.

---

## 1. Art Nouveau

- Sinuous organic curves and whiplash lines as primary compositional force
- Botanical motifs — intertwining vines, blossoming flowers, peacock feathers
- Ornate arched frames and decorative border panels top and bottom
- Flat decorative surfaces layered with intricate linework
- Gilded accents and subtle metallic sheen
- Muted rich palette — sage green, dusty gold, warm ivory, muted teal
- Symmetrical yet organic composition
- Stained glass and poster art quality
- Natural forms elevated into high decorative ornament

---

## 2. Bauhaus

- Strict geometric grid structure as foundation of all composition
- Primary colors only — red, yellow, blue, black, white, grey
- Flat bold color blocking with zero gradients or shading
- Asymmetric yet mathematically balanced composition
- Sans-serif typography treated as a design element, not decoration
- Circles, triangles, and rectangles as core visual vocabulary
- No decorative ornament of any kind
- Industrial clarity and functional purpose
- Form follows function as guiding aesthetic principle

---

## 3. Italian Renaissance Classicism

- Single point linear perspective creating deep architectural space
- Classical columns, arches, pilasters, and coffered ceilings as framing elements
- Warm golden fresco tones — ochre, sienna, ivory, soft blue
- Balanced symmetrical composition with central focal hierarchy
- Draped fabric and marble texture throughout
- Sfumato — soft atmospheric blurring of edges and transitions
- Heavenly or directional light descending from upper frame
- Monumental human figures scaled against grand architecture
- Order, harmony, and idealized proportion throughout

---

## 4. Cubism

- Subject fragmented into geometric planes showing multiple viewpoints simultaneously
- Angular faceted surfaces overlapping and interlocking across picture plane
- Flattened picture plane — no single fixed perspective point
- Muted earth palette — ochre, grey, brown, olive, slate
- Collage fragments — newspaper text, wood grain texture embedded in surface
- Intersecting lines breaking recognizable form into abstract shards
- Analytical deconstruction of subject into component visual information
- Tension between representation and abstraction

---

## 5. Fauvism

- Violently expressive non-naturalistic color choices
- Hot magenta, electric blue, raw orange, acid green applied without hesitation
- Bold flat color strokes with no blending or gradual transition
- Simplified flat forms defined by heavy visible outlines
- Color used to convey emotion and energy, not describe reality
- Wild contrast between complementary color pairings
- Visible energetic brushwork — spontaneous and instinctive
- Joyful, liberated, anti-academic surface energy

---

## 6. Futurism

- Diagonal aggressive compositions that break static visual balance
- Dynamic lines of force radiating outward suggesting speed and momentum
- Fragmented repeated forms implying movement through time
- Mechanical and industrial subject matter — machines, vehicles, crowds
- Overlapping motion blur dissolving solid form
- Metallic silver, electric blue, and sharp red palette
- Energy and velocity treated as the primary visual subject
- Celebration of modernity, technology, and forward motion

---

## 7. Magical Realism

- Hyperrealistic environment rendered with meticulous photographic detail
- Impossible or supernatural element integrated seamlessly as if entirely ordinary
- Lush tropical, rural, or grand architectural settings
- Warm cinematic golden hour lighting throughout
- Muted earthy tones with one luminous or surreal color accent
- Dreamlike stillness and quiet despite impossible content
- Narrative ambiguity — viewer cannot determine real from mythic
- Cultural and folkloric symbolism embedded in scene

---

## 8. Impressionism

- Loose broken visible brushstrokes as primary surface texture
- Light captured at a specific transient moment — never permanent
- Soft flickering dab and stroke texture across entire canvas
- Hard edges dissolved by atmospheric light and color
- Palette of soft lavender, pale gold, warm rose, sky blue, muted green
- Color and light prioritized over precise line and detail
- Spontaneous outdoor quality — plein air atmosphere
- Form suggested rather than defined

---

## 9. Neoclassicism

- Severe architectural symmetry based on Greek and Roman orders
- Cool restrained palette — stone white, marble grey, pale blue, gold
- Hard precise outlines with sculptural clarity and no atmospheric blur
- Heroic scale figures positioned against monumental architectural background
- Frieze-like horizontal composition — narrative reads left to right
- Rational geometric order and mathematical proportion
- Noble, austere atmosphere with zero decorative excess
- Moral seriousness and civic virtue as implied content

---

## 10. Pop Art

- Bold flat Ben-Day halftone dot screen printing texture
- High contrast primary and fluorescent color combinations
- Thick black outlines around all simplified forms
- Commercial advertising and mass media imagery as direct subject matter
- Mechanical repetition of singular image as compositional pattern
- Halftone dot overlay as both texture and printing reference
- Ironic consumer culture and celebrity energy
- Clean industrial print aesthetic — no handmade quality visible

---

## 11. Post-Impressionism

- Bold expressive color that moves beyond naturalism toward emotion
- Thick impasto paint texture building visible physical surface relief
- Visible deliberate structured brushwork — short dabs, swirling strokes, mosaic tiles
- Strong dark outlines separating flat color zones from one another
- Symbolic and emotional use of color rather than descriptive
- Tension between flat decorative surface and implied three-dimensional form
- Subjective interpretation of subject over objective representation

---

## 12. Primitivism

- Bold simplified flat forms with no perspective, depth, or shading
- Strong black outlines enclosing flat unmodulated color fills
- Tribal and folk pattern motifs used as decorative surface texture
- Earthy palette — terracotta, ochre, deep red, black, raw umber
- Raw instinctive mark-making — deliberate rejection of academic refinement
- Symmetrical mask-like geometric treatment of faces and figures
- Pattern and repetition prioritized over naturalistic representation
- Ceremonial, ritualistic, and spiritual visual energy

---

## 13. Rococo

- Exuberant asymmetrical ornament — scrolls, shells, cartouches, cherubs cascading across surface
- Pastel palette — powder blue, blush pink, soft gold, cream, pale lilac
- Frothy gilded decorative excess covering all available surface
- Curved forms throughout — straight lines avoided entirely
- Playful, sensuous, theatrical atmosphere
- Lush garden, boudoir, and interior architectural settings
- Soft diffused light with no harsh shadows or dramatic contrast
- Lightness, pleasure, and aristocratic leisure as implied subject

---

## 14. Suprematism

- Pure geometric abstraction — squares, circles, rectangles, crosses
- Forms float freely on infinite white ground with no anchoring
- Primary colors — black, red, occasionally blue — against white negative space
- No reference to the natural or representational world whatsoever
- Completely flat — zero texture, shading, or dimensional illusion
- Forms arranged to create spatial tension and dynamic visual balance
- Radical reduction to the most essential visual elements possible
- Spiritual and philosophical purity as design intention

---

## 15. Ukiyo-e

- Flat areas of bold unmodulated color separated by precise confident outlines
- Woodblock print texture with subtle ink grain and registration feel
- Asymmetric dynamic composition — subject rarely centered
- Deep indigo Prussian blue with red ochre, black, and cream palette
- Atmospheric mist and white negative space used to imply spatial depth
- Bold graphic silhouette of natural elements — waves, mountains, pine, cherry blossom
- Fabric and textile patterns rendered as flat decorative surface, not dimensional cloth
- Seasonal and poetic atmosphere — transient beauty as subject
