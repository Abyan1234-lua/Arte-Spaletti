# Character Poses — Cinematic & Iconic Reference

A reference guide for 25 iconic cinematic and cultural poses, covering body position, camera angle, and compositional notes for vertical poster format.

---

## HEROIC & POWER

### 1. The Superman
- **Pose** — figure floating vertically, arms relaxed at sides, chin lifted, chest open and forward, cape or coat hanging straight down
- **Body** — upright, elongated, weight evenly distributed, shoulders back and wide, completely still
- **Camera Angle** — low angle looking up, viewer positioned below figure — emphasizes dominance and elevation
- **Poster Notes** — figure occupies upper two-thirds of frame, sky or void below, negative space beneath figure amplifies floating quality

### 2. The T-Pose Cross
- **Pose** — figure standing upright, both arms extended fully horizontal at shoulder height, palms facing down or forward
- **Body** — perfectly symmetrical, feet together or slightly apart, head level, spine straight
- **Camera Angle** — straight on eye level or slight low angle — symmetry must be preserved, never shot from side
- **Poster Notes** — cruciform shape naturally fills wide horizontal space, works best centered on vertical format with strong negative space above and below

### 3. The Wanderer
- **Pose** — figure standing with full back to viewer, facing away toward a vast landscape, horizon, or void
- **Body** — relaxed but upright, coat or cloak optional but adds silhouette weight, arms at sides or one hand raised slightly
- **Camera Angle** — eye level from behind, slightly low — viewer stands with the figure, shares their perspective
- **Poster Notes** — figure positioned in lower third, landscape or void fills upper two-thirds, figure acts as scale anchor against vastness

### 4. The Contrapposto
- **Pose** — classical weight-shift stance, weight on one leg, opposite hip raised, slight torso twist, one shoulder lower than other
- **Body** — relaxed naturalistic S-curve through spine, not rigid or symmetrical, head tilted slightly
- **Camera Angle** — slight three-quarter angle, eye level — captures the twist and depth of the pose
- **Poster Notes** — figure fills vertical format naturally, slight diagonal of body creates movement without action, works well centered or offset to one side

### 5. The One Foot Raise
- **Pose** — one foot planted on elevated surface — rock, step, crate, rubble — forearm resting on raised knee, body leaning slightly forward
- **Body** — relaxed confidence, weight shifted to raised leg, chest open, head level or slightly down
- **Camera Angle** — low angle, slightly to the side — elevated foot and dominant posture amplified by low perspective
- **Poster Notes** — diagonal line from raised foot to head creates strong compositional axis, works well with environmental context visible below figure

### 6. The Crossed Arms
- **Pose** — figure standing upright, arms folded across chest, direct unbroken eye contact with viewer
- **Body** — feet planted wide, shoulders square, no lean or twist — absolute stillness and certainty
- **Camera Angle** — straight on eye level or very slight low angle — confrontational and direct
- **Poster Notes** — compact symmetrical silhouette, works centered in frame, strong negative space on either side amplifies authority and self-containment

## ACTION & DYNAMIC

### 7. The Matrix Dodge
- **Pose** — extreme backbend with upper body parallel to ground, one leg planted firmly, other extended, arms trailing or reaching
- **Body** — spine arched to impossible degree, weight on single planted foot, head tilted back, body forming dramatic curve
- **Camera Angle** — low side angle or dramatic Dutch tilt — captures the full arc of the backbend
- **Poster Notes** — horizontal body cutting across vertical format creates maximum tension, works best with motion blur or environmental detail to sell speed

### 8. The Leap of Faith
- **Pose** — figure in free-fall or jump, arms spread open wide, head back or forward, body fully open and exposed
- **Body** — no tension — completely surrendered to the fall, fabric and hair caught in upward rush of air
- **Camera Angle** — low angle looking up at falling figure, or bird's eye looking down — both create vertigo
- **Poster Notes** — figure centrally placed with massive void above or below, scale of fall implied by emptiness around figure

### 9. The Gunslinger
- **Pose** — figure in strict side profile, arm fully extended forward with weapon, opposite arm back for balance, legs planted in wide stance
- **Body** — taut and precise, total focus forward, one eye closed or narrowed, perfectly still at peak of aim
- **Camera Angle** — strict side profile, eye level — profile must be clean and unobstructed
- **Poster Notes** — strong horizontal axis from extended arm creates tension across vertical frame, figure positioned in center or lower third

### 10. The Charging Run
- **Pose** — full sprint posture leaning aggressively forward, arms pumping, one foot fully off ground, forward momentum extreme
- **Body** — entire body angled forward at dramatic lean, face set with intensity, coat or hair streaming behind
- **Camera Angle** — low angle from slightly in front — figure appears to run toward and over the viewer
- **Poster Notes** — diagonal lean of running figure cuts through vertical format dynamically, motion blur on extremities sells speed

### 11. The Mid-Strike
- **Pose** — fist, weapon, or limb caught at absolute peak of impact — the exact frame of contact or maximum extension
- **Body** — full body torque driving the strike, weight fully committed, non-striking arm back for counterbalance
- **Camera Angle** — low angle slightly to the side of the strike — captures full body commitment and impact force
- **Poster Notes** — strike arm or weapon points toward viewer or across frame creating strong directional energy, dust or impact particle effects reinforce the moment

### 12. The Slide Stop
- **Pose** — figure sliding into frame on one knee, other leg extended behind, one hand touching ground for balance, dust or debris trailing
- **Body** — low to ground, momentum still visible in body angle, head up and forward looking
- **Camera Angle** — low angle at ground level, slight side angle — captures the slide trajectory and ground-level drama
- **Poster Notes** — horizontal low composition works well in lower third of poster, sky or environment fills upper space above the slide

## CONTEMPLATIVE & EMOTIONAL

### 13. The Thinker
- **Pose** — seated or perched, elbow resting on knee, fist or knuckles pressed against chin or cheek, gaze downward or unfocused
- **Body** — weight forward, slight hunch, inward and contained energy, body language suggesting deep interior thought
- **Camera Angle** — slight low angle, three-quarter view — captures both the face and the compressed body posture
- **Poster Notes** — compact contained silhouette works well off-center, large negative space around figure amplifies solitude and thought

### 14. The Silhouette Stare
- **Pose** — figure standing at threshold — window, doorway, open landscape — backlit by strong light source, face in shadow or partial profile
- **Body** — still, weight evenly distributed, arms at sides, body framed precisely within the threshold opening
- **Camera Angle** — straight on from behind or slight side — threshold must frame the figure cleanly
- **Poster Notes** — threshold acts as natural frame within the poster frame, strong backlight creates dramatic silhouette, works best with rich texture or landscape visible through opening

### 15. The Over Shoulder
- **Pose** — figure mid-walk or stopped, body angled away from viewer, single glance back over one shoulder, expression visible
- **Body** — weight slightly forward as if interrupted mid-departure, one foot ahead, slight torso twist to enable the look back
- **Camera Angle** — eye level from slightly behind and to the side — captures both the departure and the returning gaze
- **Poster Notes** — diagonal body line and returning gaze create narrative tension, viewer is implicated as the reason the figure looks back

### 16. The Kneel of Grief
- **Pose** — both knees on ground, head bowed low or hands pressed to ground, body collapsed inward, no resistance left
- **Body** — total surrender of posture, spine curved, shoulders forward, arms either reaching down or wrapped around self
- **Camera Angle** — eye level or slight high angle looking down — high angle amplifies vulnerability and smallness
- **Poster Notes** — low grounded figure with large empty space above creates powerful weight, rain, ash, or particle atmosphere overhead amplifies emotional register

### 17. The Reach
- **Pose** — one arm fully extended upward, fingers spread or grasping toward something above, body slightly lifted onto toes, face turned upward
- **Body** — full vertical extension, entire body oriented upward, other arm trailing or pressed to side
- **Camera Angle** — low angle looking up along the extended arm — captures both the striving figure and what lies above
- **Poster Notes** — strong vertical axis of reaching arm reinforces poster format, light source or object of desire positioned at top of frame toward which figure reaches

## SEATED & GROUNDED

### 18. The Throne
- **Pose** — seated centrally and upright, both hands resting on armrests, feet flat and planted, gaze level and direct
- **Body** — completely still, perfectly symmetrical, absolute authority in every line, no casual lean or slouch
- **Camera Angle** — slight low angle, straight on — viewer looks up slightly to the seated figure, reinforcing power
- **Poster Notes** — throne and figure form natural vertical composition, architectural framing of throne echoes poster border, figure centered with symmetrical negative space either side

### 19. The Floor Sit
- **Pose** — seated cross-legged directly on ground, hands resting on knees or in lap, spine upright or gently relaxed, gaze forward or downward
- **Body** — grounded and settled, no urgency, body language suggesting patience, meditation, or quiet resolve
- **Camera Angle** — eye level from ground, slight three-quarter angle — viewer is brought down to figure's level
- **Poster Notes** — low placement of figure in frame with large environment above, ground texture and negative space work together, works well in wide natural or architectural settings

### 20. The Perch
- **Pose** — seated on edge of high surface — rooftop ledge, beam, branch — legs dangling freely, one or both hands gripping edge lightly
- **Body** — relaxed casual ease despite dangerous height, weight slightly back, leaning on hands, gaze outward or downward
- **Camera Angle** — slight low angle from below and in front — emphasizes height and the void below dangling feet
- **Poster Notes** — figure in upper third of poster, vast urban or natural void below, height and drop implied by empty lower frame

### 21. The Crouch
- **Pose** — deeply coiled low position, both feet flat on ground, knees fully bent, weight forward on balls of feet, fingertips touching ground for balance
- **Body** — compressed potential energy, animal readiness, head up and alert, body taut and prepared
- **Camera Angle** — low angle at near-ground level, slight side or front — captures compressed power and readiness
- **Poster Notes** — low horizontal mass of crouched figure creates tension against vertical format, works well with strong ground texture and environment looming above

## CINEMATIC SIGNATURE

### 22. The Bride
- **Pose** — figure walking directly forward, object held in front — bouquet, weapon, or symbolic item — veil, coat, or train trailing behind
- **Body** — deliberate measured stride, head level, gaze forward and unwavering, movement ceremonial and purposeful
- **Camera Angle** — low angle straight on — figure walks toward viewer with full frontal presence
- **Poster Notes** — strong central vertical axis, trailing elements fill lower frame behind figure, works powerfully with motion and atmospheric depth

### 23. The Last Stand
- **Pose** — lone figure from behind, standing still and upright, facing an implied vast oncoming threat ahead, weapon at side or raised
- **Body** — still, shoulders set, no flinch or retreat, total commitment in the posture despite impossible odds
- **Camera Angle** — low angle from slightly behind — viewer stands with the figure, shares the weight of what lies ahead
- **Poster Notes** — figure in lower center, threat or void fills upper two-thirds, scale of what approaches implied by negative space and atmospheric elements

### 24. The Pietà
- **Pose** — one upright figure cradling a second fallen or limp figure in arms or across lap, one face upward in anguish, one face peaceful or downturned
- **Body** — supporting figure bears full weight of the held figure, body curved around them protectively, grief visible in every line
- **Camera Angle** — eye level or slight low angle, three-quarter view — captures both faces and the full weight of the held figure
- **Poster Notes** — two-figure composition creates strong triangular mass, works centered in frame, negative space above amplifies grief and loss

### 25. The Reveal Turn
- **Pose** — figure caught mid-rotation, body turning from away to facing viewer, coat or cape caught dramatically mid-swing, face just becoming visible
- **Body** — weight on turning foot, momentum of spin still visible in fabric and hair, expression just emerging from shadow into light
- **Camera Angle** — slight low angle, three-quarter from the front — catches the mid-swing of fabric and the emerging face simultaneously
- **Poster Notes** — swinging coat or cape creates dramatic diagonal sweep across frame, works best with strong backlight creating rim light on turning figure
